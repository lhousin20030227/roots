# PLAYER INTERACTION

## Purpose

This document defines every way the player interacts with the world of ROOTS.

Interactions should feel natural, responsive, and meaningful.

---

# Interaction Philosophy

Every interaction must answer at least one of these questions:

* Does it reveal information?
* Does it support the story?
* Does it solve a puzzle?
* Does it increase immersion?

If the answer is **No**, the interaction should not exist.

---

# Interaction Types

## Inspect

The player closely examines an object.

Examples:

* Photographs
* Letters
* Furniture
* Decorations
* Personal belongings

Inspection may reveal hidden details.

---

## Pick Up

The player collects important items.

Examples:

* Keys
* Documents
* Evidence
* Personal objects
* Puzzle items

Collected items are automatically stored.

---

## Read

Used for written information.

Examples:

* Letters
* Books
* Diaries
* Notes
* Official documents

Reading should never interrupt gameplay with unnecessarily long animations.

---

## Listen

Used for audio-based evidence.

Examples:

* Voice Recordings
* Cassette Tapes
* Answering Machines
* Radio Messages

Players can replay recordings at any time.

---

## Open / Close

Examples:

* Doors
* Drawers
* Cabinets
* Windows
* Boxes

Locked objects require logical solutions.

---

## Use

The player uses an inventory item on a valid object.

Examples:

* Use a key on a locked door.
* Use a code on a keypad.
* Insert a cassette into a recorder.

Only logical item combinations are allowed.

---

## Talk

The player interacts with NPCs.

Dialogue changes based on:

* Story progress
* Collected evidence
* Previous conversations
* Relationship level

---

## Examine Environment

The player observes the surroundings without picking anything up.

Environmental clues include:

* Furniture placement
* Lighting
* Damage
* Dust
* Missing objects
* Family photographs

---

# Interaction Feedback

Every interaction should provide immediate feedback through:

* Animation
* Sound
* Character reaction
* UI confirmation (only when necessary)

The player should always know that the interaction succeeded.

---

# Interaction Priority

When multiple objects are nearby:

1. Story-critical object.
2. Puzzle object.
3. Evidence.
4. Interactive furniture.
5. Decorative object.

This prevents accidental interactions.

---

# Design Rules

* No meaningless interactions.
* No fake interactive objects.
* No interaction spam.
* Keep interaction prompts simple.
* Respect player immersion.

---

# Final Rule

Every interaction should make the player feel like a real investigator inside a believable world. Interactions are not there to increase playtime—they exist to deepen discovery, storytelling, and immersion.
