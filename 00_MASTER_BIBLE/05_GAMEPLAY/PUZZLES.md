# PUZZLES

## Purpose

The Puzzle System provides meaningful challenges that reinforce investigation, observation, and storytelling.

Puzzles should always feel like natural problems that exist within the world.

---

# Puzzle Philosophy

Every puzzle must have a believable reason to exist.

The player solves problems because they are part of the investigation—not because the game places artificial obstacles in the way.

---

# Puzzle Types

## Search Puzzles

The player carefully explores locations to find important items.

Examples:

* Hidden key
* Missing document
* Old photograph
* Personal diary

---

## Logic Puzzles

The player connects multiple clues to reach a conclusion.

Examples:

* Matching names with dates.
* Connecting family photographs.
* Reconstructing a timeline.
* Identifying inconsistencies between documents.

---

## Lock Puzzles

Access is restricted until the player finds the correct solution.

Examples:

* Key-operated locks.
* Combination locks.
* Password-protected computers.
* Filing cabinet locks.

The solution must always exist somewhere in the game world.

---

## Reconstruction Puzzles

The player restores incomplete information.

Examples:

* Reassemble torn documents.
* Restore damaged photographs.
* Organize scattered files.
* Arrange evidence in chronological order.

---

## Environmental Puzzles

The environment itself contains the solution.

Examples:

* Furniture hiding a secret compartment.
* A loose floorboard.
* A painting concealing a safe.
* A light switch revealing a hidden room.

Observation is more important than trial and error.

---

# Difficulty Principles

Puzzles should challenge the player's:

* Observation
* Memory
* Logic
* Deduction

They should never depend on:

* Fast reactions
* Random guessing
* Perfect timing

---

# Hint System

Hints are progressive.

Level 1:
A subtle observation.

Level 2:
A clearer direction.

Level 3:
A strong hint without giving the full solution.

Hints are optional and never forced.

---

# Story Integration

Every major puzzle must reveal at least one of the following:

* New evidence
* Character development
* World building
* Story progression
* Emotional impact

Solving a puzzle should always feel rewarding.

---

# Puzzle Rules

* Every puzzle has one logical solution.
* Solutions must be discoverable through gameplay.
* No impossible puzzles.
* No random code generation.
* No puzzles requiring outside knowledge.
* No repeated puzzle designs without variation.

---

# Forbidden Puzzle Types

Do not include:

* Sliding tile puzzles.
* Match-3 puzzles.
* Memory card games.
* Sudoku-style puzzles.
* Reflex or timing challenges.
* Random combination guessing.
* Arbitrary fetch quests disguised as puzzles.

---

# Final Rule

Every puzzle in ROOTS should make the player feel like a real investigator. The satisfaction comes from understanding the world and connecting clues—not from overcoming artificial difficulty.
